Introduction to Vehicle Forensics

A first introduction into the world of vehicle forensics.

What is Vehicle Forensics?

Vehicle forensics is the practice of applying digital forensic techniques to vehicles. This allows investigators to extract and analyse valuable data out of vehicles, which can be crucial during an investigation.

What data can be found in a vehicle?

  • ✅ Speed, braking and movement data
  • ✅ GPS location data and search history
  • ✅ Black box (EDR) crash data
  • ✅ Telematic data

History of Vehicle Forensics

Image Source: Unsplash


Early Stages (Pre-1990s)

Early vehicle investigations relied solely on physical evidence, such as tire marks and skid patterns. There was no digital data available to investigators.

1990s - EDRs Introduced

In 1994, General Motors developed the first airbag control modules, that recorded crash data such as speed, braking and seatbelt status.

In 1996, The United States mandated OBD-II ports in all vehicles, enabling standardised vehicle diagnostics.

Early EDRs stored 5-15 seconds of pre crash data, that was mainly used by manufacturers not law enforcement.

2000s - Legal Recognition and Standardisation

In 2000, The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) began studying EDRs for crash investigations.

In 2006, The NHTSA standardised EDR data and made it a requirement in all U.S light vehicles by 2014

In 2008, Toyota's Sudden Inteded Acceleration issue, discussed in the case studies part of the site, highlighted the need for forensic ECU analysis.

Infotainment systems became a key evidence source in criminal investigations.

2010s - Rise of Connected Cars

In 2013, Tesla introduced over-the-air updates, which prompted questions regarding the security of it's cloud based data

In 2017, The EU's eCall mandate required automatic crash, expanding telematic forensics.

In 2019, First autonomous vehicle fatality investigations.
